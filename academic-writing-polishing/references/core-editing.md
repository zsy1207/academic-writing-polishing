## Principles

- Good academic writing communicates an idea clearly and efficiently.
- Clarity matters more than elegance.
- Writing is a learned skill. Expect multiple revision passes.
- Prefer plain precision to impressive-sounding vagueness.
- In polishing work, fix the root rhetorical problem before polishing the sentence surface.
- Revise until the passage is safe, clear, and fit for purpose; do not keep polishing only to chase stylistic variation.

## Cut clutter

### Remove the usual clutter

- Dead phrases:
  - `it should be emphasized that`
  - `as it is well known`
  - `it can be regarded that`
- Empty modifiers:
  - `important`
  - `methodologic`
  - `basic tenets`
- Wordy phrases that can become short verbs or nouns
- Unnecessary jargon and unexplained acronyms
- Repeated pairs that say the same thing twice
- Weak adverbs such as `very`, `really`, `quite`, `basically`, and `generally`

### Prefer positive constructions when they are clearer

- `did not remember` ‚Üí `forgot`
- `did not succeed` ‚Üí `failed`
- `does not have` ‚Üí `lacks`

Do not force positivity when the negative form is genuinely more precise.

### Replace empty openings

- Replace `there is/there are/there was` with a real subject whenever possible.
- Replace vague openings with the actor, variable, or finding.

### Omit needless prepositions and filler words

- `on Monday` ‚Üí `Monday`
- `agreed that it was true` ‚Üí `agreed it was true`

## Work with verbs

### Prefer active voice by default

- Active voice is usually clearer, shorter, and less ambiguous.
- To convert passive to active, ask: "WHO does what to whom?" and make that agent the subject.
- Use passive voice sparingly and purposefully.
- Passive voice is often acceptable in Methods, where the procedure matters more than the actor.

### Personal pronouns are acceptable

- `we` and `I` do not make science less objective.
- Use them when they clarify responsibility for claims, choices, or interpretations.

### Choose strong verbs

- Verbs carry the sentence.
- Replace weak verb plus adverb combinations with one stronger verb when possible.
- Use `to be` verbs purposefully, not as the default engine of every sentence.

### Avoid nominalizations

Academic prose often hides action inside nouns.

- `provide an explanation of` ‚Üí `explain`
- `conduct an analysis of` ‚Üí `analyze`
- `make a recommendation` ‚Üí `recommend`

### Keep the main verb visible

- Keep the subject and main verb close together.
- Avoid long noun strings or interruptions before the sentence gets to its action.

## Build better sentences and paragraphs

### Use punctuation deliberately

Separating power (weakest ‚Üí strongest): comma ‚Üí colon ‚Üí dash ‚Üí parentheses ‚Üí semicolon ‚Üí period.
Formality (least ‚Üí most formal): dash ‚Üí parentheses ‚Üí comma, colon, semicolon.

- Comma: light separation.
- Colon: explanation, list, conclusion, or amplification. Can join two independent clauses when the second extends the first.
- Dash: stronger emphasis or interruption ‚Äî "stronger than a comma, less formal than a colon, more relaxed than parentheses." Do not overuse or the emphasis disappears.
- Parentheses: background detail the reader may skip. If removed, the sentence should still stand.
- Semicolon: joins two independent clauses or separates complex list items.

### Vary sentence length and structure

- Alternate short and long sentences to create natural rhythm.
- Avoid chains of identically structured sentences (e.g., every sentence starting with the subject followed by a weak verb).
- A short declarative sentence after a long, complex one creates emphasis.
- Use different sentence openings: a dependent clause, a prepositional phrase, or a transitional word can break monotony without adding clutter.

### Maintain parallelism

- Items joined by `and`, `or`, or `but` should match grammatically.
- Lists should stay in one grammatical pattern.
- Choose a structure and stick to it.

### Build paragraphs around one idea

- One paragraph should carry one controlling idea.
- Keep paragraphs short enough to stay focused ‚Äî shorter paragraphs give more white space and feel less tedious.
- Put the key sentence early; readers remember the first and last sentences best.
- Make first and last sentences count: the first should orient, the last should land the point.

### Edit paragraphs from the outline up

Before rewriting sentences, tag each existing paragraph with a one-sentence summary of its job. Reorder, merge, or split paragraphs to restore logical flow first. Only then polish sentences within the stabilized structure. This prevents wasted effort on sentences that will move or disappear.

### Prefer logical flow over transition-word overload

Good paragraph flow comes from order, not from stacking connectors.

Useful default patterns:
- general ‚Üí specific
- question ‚Üí answer
- claim ‚Üí evidence
- time sequence

Acceptable high-frequency transitions: `and`, `or`, `but`, `however`. Reserve heavier transitions (`moreover`, `furthermore`, `additionally`, `nevertheless`) for genuine logical pivots. If a paragraph needs many transition words to make sense, the underlying order is probably wrong.

### Synthesize literature, do not summarize study-by-study

When discussing prior work (Introduction, Discussion), compare, contrast, and critique across studies rather than describing them one-by-one.

- "Three studies found X, but two others found Y, possibly because of methodological differences" is stronger than separate summaries of each study.
- Organizing by theme or finding reveals patterns and gaps. Organizing by paper produces a list of facts.
- In the Introduction, summarize prior work at a high level; save detailed comparisons and critique for the Discussion.

### Use lists and series deliberately

- Use a list only when it clarifies structure or comparison.
- Prefer the smallest list that fully supports the point.
- Two items are often enough; three can help rhythm when the content naturally comes in three parts.
- Do not force tricolons or balanced cadences just for style. In scientific prose, formulaic triplets can sound generic or AI-written.

### Repeat key terms when needed

- Scientific writing often benefits from repeating the right keyword.
- Do not reach for a synonym just to avoid repetition if the synonym creates doubt.

### Use acronyms sparingly

- Introduce only standard or high-value acronyms.
- Define them clearly.
- Redefine long-forgotten acronyms in long documents when needed.

### Prefer tables or figures when prose is the wrong container

If the information is fundamentally hierarchical, procedural, or heavily numeric, consider a table, figure, or diagram instead of forcing it into a dense paragraph.

## Logic and argumentation

### Ensure every argument has a clear logical chain

Each claim should connect to its evidence through explicit steps. Readers should never need to infer a missing premise.

Common logical patterns in scientific writing:
- **Deductive**: General principle + specific case ‚Üí conclusion.
- **Inductive**: Specific observations ‚Üí general pattern.
- **Claim ‚Üí evidence ‚Üí implication**: State the finding, cite the support, draw the bounded conclusion.

### Avoid logical fallacies

Watch for:
- Post hoc ergo propter hoc: correlation presented as causation.
- False equivalence: treating unequal bodies of evidence as equal.
- Hasty generalization: overclaiming from limited data.
- Appeal to authority without evidence: "Smith showed X" when Smith only suggested X.
- Straw man in Discussion: misrepresenting prior work to make own findings look better.

### Match qualifiers to evidence type

- Randomized controlled trial ‚Üí stronger language (`demonstrated`, `showed`).
- Observational study ‚Üí hedged language (`was associated with`, `suggests`).
- Preliminary or pilot data ‚Üí cautious language (`may indicate`, `warrants further investigation`).
- Meta-analysis ‚Üí depends on heterogeneity and quality of included studies.

### Professional precision in word choice

Choose the exact word, not the approximate one:
- `significant` means statistically significant, not "important" or "large."
- `prove` is reserved for mathematics; use `demonstrate` or `show` for empirical findings.
- `correlation` ‚âÝ `association` ‚âÝ `causation` ‚Äî use the correct term for the study design.
- `novel` means genuinely first; do not use it for incremental advances.
- `parameter` ‚âÝ `variable`; `accuracy` ‚âÝ `precision` ‚Äî use the term whose technical definition matches the context.
- `reports` vs `estimates` vs `projects` ‚Äî choose the verb that matches the nature of the claim (observed fact, statistical inference, or forecast).
- `prevalence` ‚âÝ `incidence`; `mortality` ‚âÝ `fatality` ‚Äî epidemiologic terms have specific meanings.

## Calibrate hedging

### When hedging is appropriate

- Observational associations that could be confounded
- Preliminary or underpowered findings
- Mechanistic speculation beyond the data

### When hedging is excessive

- Stacking multiple hedges in one sentence: `It is possible that X may potentially be related to‚Ä¶`
- Hedging well-established facts or direct measurements
- Using vague attribution (`It has been suggested‚Ä¶`) when a specific citation is available

Safe revision pattern:
- Keep one hedge per claim.
- Choose the hedge that best matches the evidence strength: `suggests` < `indicates` < `demonstrates` < `shows` < `establishes`.
- Remove empty hedges that add no epistemic information.

## Present numbers effectively

- Prefer percent change, ratio, or comparison statements over raw duplication of table values in Results prose.
- Use absolute risk rather than relative risk when writing for broad audiences or when relative risk could mislead (e.g., "8 additional cases per 10,000 women" rather than "26% increased risk").
- Present whole numbers over tiny percentages for non-specialist readers.
- Round to meaningful precision; do not add false precision.

## Micro-grammar reminders

- `data` is plural in formal scientific prose.
- `affect` is usually the verb; `effect` is usually the noun.
- `compared with` usually highlights differences among similar things; `compared to` usually highlights similarity across unlike things.
- Use `that` for restrictive clauses and `which` for nonrestrictive clauses.
- Avoid singular antecedents paired with `they` or `their` when the sentence can be rewritten cleanly in the plural.

## Numerical consistency and reference integrity

### Check numbers across sections

- Do numbers in the abstract match those in the text, tables, and figures?
- Do numbers in the text match those in the tables and figures?
- Do numbers across tables and figures agree with each other?
- Are sample sizes consistent across sections (e.g., Methods says N=500 but a table sums to 498)?

Inconsistencies between sections are among the most common errors in published papers and a frequent trigger for reviewer criticism.

### Check reference integrity

- Verify that cited references actually contain the information attributed to them.
- Prefer primary sources over secondary sources; information often gets distorted when cited through intermediaries.
- Watch for misnumbered or misplaced references ‚Äî a common error during revision.
- Assume that other authors may have introduced errors when citing sources; do not propagate claims uncritically.

## Optional process coaching

Treat writing as three distinct stages.

### 1. Pre-writing

- Collect and organize evidence before drafting prose.
- Build a rough outline or paragraph roadmap.
- Decide on take-home messages away from the screen when useful.
- Group like ideas together before sentences exist.

Rough time split:
- pre-writing: about 70%
- first draft: about 10%
- revision: about 20%

### 2. First draft

- Write quickly.
- Focus on getting the logic and facts down in complete sentences.
- Do not try to perfect every sentence while the structure is still unstable.

### 3. Revision

Run multiple focused checks rather than one vague polish pass.

- Prioritize substantive issues over cosmetic restyling.
- Stop once no substantive issue remains; one or two focused rounds usually outperform endless micro-edits.
- Read aloud (or simulate reading aloud) to catch awkward rhythm, monotonous sentence patterns, and unintended repetition.
- Do a verb check:
  - weak `to be` verbs
  - passive constructions
  - buried verbs (subject and main verb too far apart)
- Cut clutter.
- Do an organizational review: tag each paragraph with its main point and reorder as needed; combine paragraphs that overlap, split those carrying two ideas.
- Check numerical consistency across abstract, text, tables, and figures.
- Verify reference integrity (see `Numerical consistency and reference integrity`).
- Get feedback from a smart outside reader when audience reach matters ‚Äî they should be able to grasp the main findings and significance without struggling.

## Example edits

Use this section as a quick in-file reminder. For the primary before/after example bank, see `academic-revision-examples.md`.

### Clutter and nominalization

Before:
> It should be emphasized that the analysis of these data provides an explanation of the observed difference.

After:
> These data explain the observed difference.

### Passive to active

Before:
> A recommendation was made by the committee that the study be halted.

After:
> The committee recommended halting the study.

### Buried verb

Before:
> One study of patients with severe asthma and prolonged corticosteroid exposure found that only a small proportion improved.

After:
> One study found that only a small proportion of patients with severe asthma and prolonged corticosteroid exposure improved.

### Paragraph focus

Before:
> The intervention reduced blood pressure in the treatment group. The hospital also changed its electronic record system during the study. The intervention was easy to deliver. Participants in the control group were older.

After:
> The intervention lowered blood pressure in the treatment group and was easy to deliver. Because the hospital changed its electronic record system during the study and the control group was older at baseline, those differences should be considered when interpreting the effect size.

### Stacked hedges

Before:
> It is possible that the observed association may be potentially related to differences in baseline diet.

After:
> The observed association may reflect differences in baseline diet.

### Numbers: comparison over raw duplication

Before:
> Table 2 shows that the treatment group exercised 60 minutes per day and the control group exercised 30 minutes per day.

After:
> Participants in the treatment group exercised twice as long as controls (Table 2).

## Final pre-delivery checklist

- Is the main point clear early enough?
- Does each paragraph do one job, with the key sentence near the top?
- Are the strongest verbs carrying the key claims?
- Do sentence lengths and structures vary enough to maintain rhythm?
- Did you cut nominalizations, empty hedging, and filler phrases?
- Is hedging calibrated ‚Äî one hedge per claim, no stacking, no stripping of justified caution?
- Are numbers presented as comparisons or summaries rather than raw table duplication?
- Are punctuation choices helping rather than distracting?
- Are repeated keywords clearer than forced synonyms?
- Is every argument logically sound ‚Äî no unsupported leaps, no missing premises?
- Are qualifiers matched to the evidence type (RCT vs observational vs preliminary)?
- Is word choice professionally precise (`significant` = statistical, `prove` = math only)?
- Is prior literature synthesized across studies, not summarized study-by-study?
- Do numbers in abstract, text, tables, and figures all agree?
- Do cited references actually contain the information claimed?
- Did you read for awkwardness and monotony, not just correctness?
