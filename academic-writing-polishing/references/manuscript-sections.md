## Recommended writing order

For original research manuscripts, a useful default order is:

1. Tables and figures
2. Results
3. Methods
4. Introduction
5. Discussion
6. Abstract

Reason:
- Tables and figures are the backbone of the story.
- Results become easier once the visual story is clear.
- Introduction and Discussion are easier once the findings are fixed.
- Abstract should summarize the finished paper, not the intended one.

## Tables and figures

### Core rule

Each table or figure should make one clear point and stand on its own.
Many editors, reviewers, and readers will inspect the abstract and displays before they read the full paper, so the story must survive that skim path.

### Prefer the right display

- Figures:
  - show trends, contrasts, patterns, or relationships
  - provide quick visual impact
- Tables:
  - provide precise values
  - handle many variables or exact comparisons

Do not present the same data in both a table and a figure unless there is a strong reason.
Use the fewest figures and tables needed to tell the story.

### Table rules

- Use a short, specific title that identifies the point of the table.
- Repeat the same key terms used in the text and column headings.
- Include units in every column header.
- Use a reasonable number of significant figures — do not add false precision.
- Follow journal formatting (most journals use three horizontal lines: above column headings, below column headings, below data; avoid grid lines).
- Use footnotes (superscript symbols) for abbreviations, experimental details, and significance markers; define all acronyms within the table.
- Align columns consistently; ensure capitalization is uniform.
- Model the table's look and feel after published tables in the target journal.
- Avoid overcrowded tables that bury the main message — if a table has too many columns, consider splitting it or moving less critical data to a supplement.

### Figure rules

- Use the legend to make the figure understandable without the main text: include a brief title, essential experimental details, symbol/line/panel definitions, and statistical information.
- Define symbols, line types, and panels.
- Use simple graphs that distinguish groups clearly (different shapes, colors, or line styles).
- Prefer tables when a graph becomes visually overloaded.
- Beware graphs that visually exaggerate small effects through axis manipulation or area distortion.

### Graph types

- Line graphs: trends over time, dose, or age.
- Bar graphs: group comparisons at one timepoint.
- Scatter plots: relationships and individual data points (more information).
- Diagrams: models, cause–effect cycles, invisible structures — replace long text.

## Results

### Job of the section

Summarize what the data show. Results ≠ raw data — the section should tell the reader the take-home messages from each table and figure, not transcribe the numbers.

Do:
- describe trends, contrasts, and relationships
- cite the supporting table or figure
- highlight only the most important numbers
- include negative and control results
- complement the table or figure with precise values or higher-value comparisons when needed
- prefer percent change, ratio, or contrast statements when they tell the story better than raw duplication
- organize subsections so each one maps to a clear display item or result cluster when possible
- use informative subheadings when they help readers follow multiple result clusters

Do not:
- waste the first sentence on `Table 1 shows` or `Figure 2 shows` when the take-home message can be stated directly
- narrate every number line by line — the reader can look at the table for specific values
- explain why the result happened (save interpretation for Discussion)
- justify analytic choices that belong in Methods
- interpret importance beyond what the data show

### Verb tense and voice

- Use **past tense** for findings and completed analyses: `We found that…`, `Women were more likely to…`
- Use **present tense** for what a table or figure shows when that remains true in the document: `Figure 1 shows…`, `The data suggest…`
- Use the term `significant` only for statistical significance.
- Prefer **active voice** when it improves clarity: `We compared…` rather than `A comparison was made…`

## Methods

### Job of the section

Give enough information for a knowledgeable reader to understand and replicate what was done.

### Typical components

- materials
- participants or subjects
- design or protocol
- measurements
- analysis

### Rules

- Break the section into informative subheadings.
- Cite standard methods instead of re-explaining them in full.
- Use tables or flow diagrams when they clarify a complex procedure.
- Report completed actions in past tense.
- Passive voice is acceptable when it keeps focus on the procedure.
- Technical language is acceptable when it improves precision.

Key question set:
- who
- what
- where
- how
- why

## Introduction

### Job of the section

Set up the research question, not the answer.

### Typical structure

A useful narrowing pattern is:
- what is known
- what remains unknown or limited
- the specific question, aim, or hypothesis
- a brief note about the approach when useful

### Rules

- Keep the Introduction focused on the exact question studied.
- Keep it compact: 2-5 short paragraphs are often enough.
- Do not turn it into a general-topic literature review.
- Keep paragraphs short.
- Write for a reasonably educated reader.
- State the aim or hypothesis explicitly.
- Do not introduce results, detailed methods, or full discussion arguments.

### Verb tense and voice

- **Past tense** and **active voice** for discussing previous results and prior studies: `Smith et al. found…`
- **Present tense** for established facts and current knowledge: `Smoking increases…`
- State the aim or hypothesis explicitly using direct phrasing:
  - `We asked whether...`
  - `Our hypothesis was...`
  - `We tested the hypothesis that...`
  - `Our aim was...`
  - `We proposed...`

## Discussion

### Job of the section

Answer the question posed by the Introduction, explain what the results mean, defend the conclusion, and show why the work matters. The Discussion gives the most freedom and the most chance to display good writing — but also the most room for common mistakes.

### Core movement

Think of the Discussion as an inverted funnel (the mirror image of the Introduction's narrowing cone):
- start narrow with the main answer to the question posed in the Introduction
- place the finding in the context of the literature
- address limitations that genuinely threaten interpretation
- end broader with implications and take-home message

### Rules

- **Start with the main finding.** The first paragraph should begin with something like `We found that…` or `This study shows that…`
- **End with the main finding or take-home message.** The reader should leave knowing the one key result and why it matters.
- **Do not start with limitations.** Bury them several paragraphs in, after you have established the answer and its context.
- Stay close to what the data actually support. Do not discuss things for which you have no data.
- Discuss the limitations that genuinely threaten interpretation, not generic filler. If a limitation matters, explain how it could affect conclusions.
- Show how the findings fit or conflict with prior work.
- Distinguish between hypothesis-driven conclusions and exploratory interpretation when both appear.
- Do not end with generic filler such as `more research is needed`; if future work matters, say what specific uncertainty remains and why resolving it matters.
- Use active voice and narrative clarity. Tell it like a story.

### Verb tense

- **Past tense** for what the study found or what prior studies reported: `We found…`, `Miller et al. found…`
- **Present tense** for what the findings suggest or mean now: `The greater weight loss suggests…`, `The explanation for this difference is not clear…`

## Abstract

### Core rule

Write the abstract last — it should summarize the finished paper, not the intended one.

### Job of the abstract

Provide a self-contained summary of the full paper. Many editors, reviewers, and readers will read only the abstract and displays.

Typical elements:
1. background (one or two sentences of context)
2. question, aim, or hypothesis
3. key methods (quick summary)
4. key results (the most important findings, with minimal raw data)
5. conclusion (the answer to the question)
6. implication or recommendation (why the reader should care)

### Rules

- Keep it short and high-level.
- Typical length is often about 100-300 words, but journal-specific rules override that default.
- Make it stand on its own.
- Prefer summary statements over raw detail.
- Include only the most important numbers — do not overload with raw values.
- Match whatever structured or unstructured format the target journal requires.
- Ensure numbers in the abstract match those in the text and tables exactly.

## Section examples

### Results example

Before:
> Table 2 shows that the treatment group exercised 60 minutes per day and the control group exercised 30 minutes per day.

After:
> Participants in the treatment group exercised twice as long as controls (Table 2).

### Discussion example

Before:
> This study has several limitations. We found that obesity was associated with increased risk of several cancers.

After:
> We found that obesity was associated with an increased risk of several cancers. This study has several limitations that should temper causal interpretation.

### Introduction example

Before:
> Breast cancer is common. Smoking is harmful. Many studies have examined smoking and cancer. In this paper, we studied breast cancer.

After:
> Prior studies suggest a possible association between smoking and breast cancer, but the evidence has been inconsistent and often limited by retrospective exposure assessment. We therefore asked whether long-term smoking exposure was associated with incident breast cancer in a prospective cohort.

### Abstract example

Before:
> We studied obesity and cancer. Many results were found. The implications are discussed.

After:
> We examined whether obesity was associated with site-specific cancer risk in a prospective cohort. Using multivariable-adjusted survival models, we found higher risks for several cancers among participants with obesity. These findings support obesity as an important correlate of cancer burden and justify further work on mechanism and prevention.

## Cover letters for journal submission

### Job

Convince the editor that the manuscript fits the journal and deserves peer review. The cover letter is the editor's first impression — many editors use it to decide whether to send the paper out for review or desk-reject it.

### Structure

1. **Opening** (1–2 sentences): State the manuscript title, type (original research, brief communication, etc.), and that you are submitting it for consideration.
2. **Key finding** (2–3 sentences): Summarize what the study found and why it matters. Lead with the result, not the background.
3. **Journal fit** (1–2 sentences): Explain specifically why this journal is the right venue — reference the journal's scope, readership, or recent related publications.
4. **Significance** (1–2 sentences): State the broader importance or novelty — what gap this work fills.
5. **Closing** (2–3 sentences): Include originality, non-duplication, author-approval, and conflict statements only if those details are already confirmed. If not, ask for them or use bracketed placeholders. Offer to suggest reviewers if the journal requests it.

### Rules

- Keep to one page, typically 3–4 short paragraphs.
- Lead with the finding, not generic praise of the journal.
- Use active voice and strong verbs throughout.
- Match the tone: professional, confident, not salesy.
- Be specific about journal fit — generic letters signal poor targeting.
- Do not repeat the abstract verbatim; the cover letter should complement it.
- Do not include detailed methods or extensive results.
- Mention any special circumstances (e.g., related manuscript under review, conference presentation, first-time author status).
- Never invent administrative declarations. If originality, author approval, conflicts, or related-manuscript status have not been confirmed, ask the user or leave a bracketed placeholder such as `[confirm competing-interest statement]`.

### Minimum template

Use this as a structural skeleton, not stock prose.

Paragraph 1:
> Dear [Editor Name or Editor],
> We submit "[manuscript title]" as a [article type] for consideration at [journal]. We report that [key finding].

Paragraph 2:
> This finding matters because [specific gap or consequence]. It is a strong fit for [journal] because [specific readership, scope, or recent conversation in the journal].

Paragraph 3:
> [Confirmed originality / non-duplication / author-approval / competing-interest statements.] If any declaration is not confirmed, keep an explicit placeholder such as `[confirm competing-interest statement]`.

Closing:
> Thank you for your consideration.
> Sincerely,
> [Corresponding author name]

Add one short sentence on special circumstances only when relevant (for example, related manuscript status, conference presentation, or suggested reviewers).

### Common mistakes

- Opening with "Dear Editor, Please find attached…" — generic and wastes the first line.
- Describing the entire study instead of highlighting the key finding.
- Failing to explain why the journal is appropriate.
- Omitting required declarations (originality, conflicts, author approval).
- Making the letter too long — editors read hundreds.

### Example phrases

- "We report that [key finding], which addresses [specific gap]."
- "These findings are directly relevant to your readership because [specific reason]."
- "This work builds on [recent publication in your journal] by [specific advance]."
- "All authors have approved the manuscript and declare no competing interests." (use only if confirmed)
- "[Confirm originality / author approval / competing-interest statement before submission]."

## Fast manuscript audit

- Do the tables and figures tell the story before the prose does?
- Does each Results subsection map to a clear display item or result cluster?
- Does the Introduction end with a precise question?
- Does the Discussion answer that question in its first paragraph?
- Did the abstract get written after the paper stabilized?
