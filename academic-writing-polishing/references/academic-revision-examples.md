# Academic Revision Examples

Use this file as a focused example bank for academic writing polishing. The goal is not to memorize stock phrases. The goal is to recognize the kinds of changes that improve academic prose without changing the underlying science.

## How to use this file

- Match the user's passage to the closest rhetorical problem (use the Quick Matching Guide at the end).
- Borrow the direction of revision, not the exact wording.
- Preserve claim strength, numbers, citations, and section role.
- Prefer the smallest rewrite that solves the real problem.
- Treat this file as the primary before/after example bank. Keep long examples here instead of duplicating them across reference files.
- For reviewer responses, stabilize the skeleton with the minimum template in `publishing-and-review.md` before borrowing tone or phrasing from Section 7.
- For cover letters, stabilize the skeleton with the minimum template in `manuscript-sections.md` before borrowing direction from Section 12.
- For paragraph restructuring, use Section 10 to see the outline-first editing method in action.
- For sentence monotony, use Section 11.

## 1. Sentence-Level Revision

### 1.1 Cut clutter

Before:
> It is important to note that there are several factors that are involved in the regulation of cell growth.

After:
> Several factors regulate cell growth.

### 1.2 Replace weak verbs

Before:
> The results are suggestive of a reduction in symptoms after treatment.

After:
> The results suggest that symptoms declined after treatment.

### 1.3 Undo nominalization

Before:
> The implementation of the intervention led to an improvement in adherence.

After:
> Implementing the intervention improved adherence.

### 1.4 Remove empty hedging

Before:
> It is possible that the observed association may be related to differences in baseline diet.

After:
> The observed association may reflect differences in baseline diet.

### 1.5 Preserve claim strength

Before:
> Obesity was associated with higher cancer risk.

Unsafe rewrite:
> Obesity increased cancer risk.

Safe rewrite:
> Obesity was associated with a higher risk of cancer.

### 1.6 Reduce AI-sounding phrasing

Before:
> These findings underscore the critical importance of a holistic and multifaceted approach to patient-centered optimization.

After:
> These findings suggest that improving care will require attention to several patient-level factors.

### 1.7 Cut clutter (physics)

Before:
> As we can see from Figure 2, if the return kinetic energy is less than 3.2 Up, there will be two electron trajectories associated with this kinetic energy.

After:
> Figure 2 shows that a return kinetic energy below 3.2 Up yields two electron trajectories.

### 1.8 Active voice (astronomy)

Before:
> The first visible-light snapshot of a planet circling another star has been taken by NASA's Hubble Space Telescope.

After:
> NASA's Hubble Space Telescope has taken the first visible-light snapshot of a planet circling another star.

### 1.9 Undo nominalization (molecular biology)

Before:
> During DNA damage, recognition of H3K4me3 by ING2 results in recruitment of Sin3/HDAC and repression of cell proliferation genes.

After:
> During DNA damage, H3K4me3 recruits ING2 and Sin3/HDAC, which together repress cell proliferation genes.

### 1.10 Cut clutter (environmental science)

Before:
> It is suspected that the importance of temperature has more to do with impacting rates of other reactions than being a mechanism of disinfection itself since ponds are rarely hot enough for temperature alone to cause disinfection.

After:
> Ponds are rarely hot enough for temperature alone to cause disinfection; thus, the effect of temperature is likely mediated through its impact on the rates of other reactions.

### 1.11 Keep protected tokens intact

Before:
> In Eq. (2), we estimated the hazard ratio as \(HR = e^{\beta_1}\), as shown in Figure 3 and Table S2.

Unsafe rewrite:
> In the next equation, we estimated risk using an exponential function, as shown in the figure and supplement.

Safe rewrite:
> As shown in Eq. (2), Figure 3, and Table S2, we estimated the hazard ratio as \(HR = e^{\beta_1}\).

## 2. Abstract Examples

### 2.1 Replace vague summary with a full mini-story

Before:
> We studied obesity and cancer. Many results were found. The implications are discussed.

After:
> We examined whether obesity was associated with site-specific cancer risk in a prospective cohort. Using multivariable-adjusted survival models, we found higher risks for several cancers among participants with obesity. These findings support obesity as an important correlate of cancer burden and justify further work on mechanism and prevention.

### 2.2 Remove excess background

Before:
> Cardiovascular disease is a major cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide and has been studied extensively for many decades in many populations. In this study, we investigated whether sleep duration was associated with hypertension in nurses.

After:
> We investigated whether sleep duration was associated with hypertension among nurses in a prospective cohort.

### 2.3 Add missing method and result

Before:
> We assessed the effect of the intervention on medication adherence. The intervention was beneficial.

After:
> We evaluated whether a text-message intervention improved medication adherence in adults with hypertension. In a randomized trial, adherence was higher in the intervention group than in usual care. These findings suggest that low-cost reminders may improve short-term adherence.

### 2.4 Remove causal overstatement

Before:
> Our findings prove that air pollution causes depression and should prompt immediate regulatory action.

After:
> Our findings suggest that higher air-pollution exposure is associated with greater depressive symptom burden and support further study of potential causal mechanisms.

### 2.5 Tighten raw-number overload

Before:
> Among 1,204 participants, 312 in group A improved, whereas 148 in group B improved, and the mean symptom score changed from 8.2 to 4.1 in group A and from 8.0 to 6.3 in group B.

After:
> Improvement was more common in group A than in group B, and symptom scores declined more sharply in group A.

## 3. Introduction Examples

### 3.1 Narrow a broad opening

Before:
> Breast cancer is common. Smoking is harmful. Many studies have examined smoking and cancer. In this paper, we studied breast cancer.

After:
> Prior studies suggest a possible association between smoking and breast cancer, but the evidence has been inconsistent and often limited by retrospective exposure assessment. We therefore asked whether long-term smoking exposure was associated with incident breast cancer in a prospective cohort.

### 3.2 Move from known to unknown

Before:
> Diabetes is increasing worldwide. Many patients have poor outcomes. We conducted a study of rural patients.

After:
> Diabetes prevalence is increasing worldwide, yet data on long-term glycemic control in rural populations remain limited. We therefore examined predictors of poor glycemic control among adults receiving primary care in rural clinics.

### 3.3 Remove results from the Introduction

Before:
> Prior studies on this topic have been mixed. In our cohort, however, we found a strong association between obesity and colorectal cancer.

After:
> Prior studies on this topic have been mixed, in part because many lacked prospective exposure assessment or adequate control for confounding. We therefore tested whether obesity was associated with incident colorectal cancer in a large prospective cohort.

### 3.4 Make the gap explicit

Before:
> Several studies have examined neighborhood deprivation and asthma. We conducted another study in children.

After:
> Several studies have examined neighborhood deprivation and asthma, but few have distinguished between incident disease and exacerbation risk in children. We therefore assessed whether neighborhood deprivation was associated with new-onset asthma and asthma-related hospitalization in a pediatric cohort.

### 3.5 Add a clear aim statement

Before:
> This issue remains controversial, and additional work is needed.

After:
> We aimed to determine whether early antibiotic exposure was associated with later obesity risk in children.

## 4. Methods Examples

### 4.1 Improve replicability

Before:
> Patients were recruited and standard methods were used to measure blood pressure.

After:
> We recruited adults from three outpatient clinics between January and June 2024. Blood pressure was measured in triplicate after 5 minutes of seated rest using an automated Omron monitor, and the mean of the final two readings was used for analysis.

### 4.2 Remove unnecessary narrative

Before:
> In order to perform the analysis, we first downloaded the data, then carefully inspected them for any unusual values, after which we proceeded with model fitting.

After:
> We downloaded the data, screened for implausible values, and then fit the prespecified models.

### 4.3 Use subheadings logic

Before:
> We enrolled participants, collected blood, measured biomarkers, and ran the analyses.

After:
> Participants, sample collection, biomarker measurement, and statistical analysis were handled in separate protocol stages.

### 4.4 Allow purposeful passive voice

Before:
> We centrifuged the samples at 3,000 rpm for 10 minutes, and then we stored them at -80 degrees C until analysis.

After:
> Samples were centrifuged at 3,000 rpm for 10 minutes and stored at -80 degrees C until analysis.

### 4.5 Replace vague analysis language

Before:
> Appropriate statistical tests were used to compare the groups.

After:
> Group differences were assessed with chi-square tests for categorical variables and t tests for continuous variables.

### 4.6 Cut buried verb (geophysics)

Before:
> After rejecting paths with poor signal-to-noise ratios, we were left with 678 velocity measurements of waves with 7.5 seconds period and 891 measurements of 15-second waves.

After:
> Rejecting paths with poor signal-to-noise ratios left 678 velocity measurements of 7.5-second waves and 891 of 15-second waves.

## 5. Results Examples

### 5.1 Do not read the table aloud

Before:
> Table 1 shows that 38% of participants in group A improved, compared with 19% in group B.

After:
> Improvement was twice as common in group A as in group B (Table 1).

### 5.2 Emphasize the comparison, not the display

Before:
> Figure 2 shows that the intervention group exercised 60 minutes per day and the control group exercised 30 minutes per day.

After:
> Participants in the intervention group exercised twice as long as controls (Figure 2).

### 5.3 Add key values only when they matter

Before:
> The mean LDL cholesterol level was 122.4 mg/dL in the intervention group and 138.1 mg/dL in the control group, as shown in Table 3.

After:
> LDL cholesterol was lower in the intervention group than in the control group (Table 3).

### 5.4 Keep interpretation out of Results

Before:
> The stronger response in women suggests that the intervention may work through hormonal pathways.

After:
> The intervention effect was larger in women than in men.

### 5.5 Report negative or control findings

Before:
> The intervention improved adherence.

After:
> The intervention improved adherence, but it did not meaningfully change blood pressure or clinic attendance.

### 5.6 Use percent change when clearer than raw duplication

Before:
> Average symptom scores decreased from 8.0 to 4.0 in group A and from 8.1 to 6.7 in group B.

After:
> Symptom scores fell by about 50% in group A but only modestly in group B.

## 6. Discussion Examples

### 6.1 Start with the answer, not the limitation

Before:
> This study has several limitations. Further research is needed. We found that treatment adherence improved.

After:
> We found that the intervention improved treatment adherence. Several limitations should qualify the strength and generalizability of that conclusion.

### 6.2 Convert generic limitation filler into a useful limitation

Before:
> Our study has some limitations, including the fact that more research is needed in the future.

After:
> Our study was limited by a short follow-up period and possible nonresponse bias, both of which may have reduced the precision of the effect estimates.

### 6.3 Reduce causal overreach

Before:
> These findings show that neighborhood deprivation leads to asthma in children.

After:
> These findings suggest that neighborhood deprivation is associated with asthma risk in children.

### 6.4 Add literature context

Before:
> Our findings were interesting and somewhat surprising.

After:
> Our findings differ from several earlier retrospective studies but align with more recent prospective analyses, suggesting that study design may explain some of the inconsistency in the literature.

### 6.5 End with a real take-home message

Before:
> More research is needed.

After:
> Together, these findings support obesity as an important correlate of cancer risk while highlighting the need for stronger causal evidence.

### 6.6 Full discussion paragraph revision

Before:
> We found that obesity was associated with increased risk of several cancers. This meta-analysis is subject to a number of limitations. There were some inconsistencies in previous studies. It is important to note that the explanation for these findings is not clear. Further research is needed.

After:
> We found that obesity was associated with an increased risk of several cancers. However, this meta-analysis has several limitations, and prior studies have not been fully consistent. The mechanisms underlying these associations remain unclear, and further research is needed to clarify them.

## 7. Reviewer-Response Examples

### 7.1 Agree and revise

Reviewer comment:
> The Discussion overstates the causal implications of the findings.

Response:
> We agree and have revised the Discussion to avoid causal language. We now state that obesity was associated with higher cancer risk, rather than claiming that obesity increased cancer risk.

### 7.2 Agree in part

Reviewer comment:
> The Introduction should discuss all prior studies in detail.

Response:
> We agree that additional context is helpful, but we aimed to keep the Introduction focused on the studies most directly relevant to the research question. We have added two sentences summarizing the most important prior prospective studies.

### 7.3 Respectful disagreement

Reviewer comment:
> The authors should remove Table 2 because the same information appears in the text.

Response:
> We respectfully disagree. Table 2 provides exact subgroup values and confidence intervals that are too detailed for the main text. To reduce redundancy, we have shortened the corresponding Results paragraph.

### 7.4 Point to the exact change

Reviewer comment:
> Please clarify how blood pressure was measured.

Response:
> We have clarified the blood-pressure protocol in the Methods section. The revised text now states that blood pressure was measured in triplicate after 5 minutes of seated rest and that the mean of the final two readings was used for analysis.

### 7.5 Fix language without conceding a scientific error

Reviewer comment:
> The statistical analysis appears inappropriate.

Response:
> We believe the analysis itself is appropriate, but we agree that the original description was too brief. We have expanded the Statistical Analysis subsection to clarify the model specification, covariate adjustment, and sensitivity analyses.

## 8. Hedging and Claim-Strength Examples

### 8.1 Remove stacked hedges

Before:
> It is possible that the observed association may be potentially related to differences in baseline diet.

After:
> The observed association may reflect differences in baseline diet.

### 8.2 Replace vague attribution with a specific citation

Before:
> It has been suggested that sleep duration affects blood pressure.

After:
> Several prospective studies suggest that short sleep duration is associated with higher blood pressure [refs].

### 8.3 Strengthen appropriately when evidence supports it

Before:
> Our findings suggest that the drug may possibly reduce mortality.

After (if from a large RCT):
> Our findings demonstrate that the drug reduced mortality.

## 9. Numbers and Data Presentation Examples

### 9.1 Use absolute risk for broad audiences

Before:
> Hormone therapy was associated with a 26% increased risk of breast cancer.

After:
> Hormone therapy was associated with 8 additional breast cancer cases per 10,000 women per year.

### 9.2 Round to meaningful precision

Before:
> The mean BMI was 27.3482 kg/m² in the intervention group and 27.1297 kg/m² in the control group.

After:
> Mean BMI was similar in both groups (about 27 kg/m²).

### 9.3 Prefer comparison over raw duplication

Before:
> Average symptom scores decreased from 8.0 to 4.0 in group A and from 8.1 to 6.7 in group B.

After:
> Symptom scores fell by about 50% in group A but only modestly in group B.

## 10. Paragraph Restructuring Examples

### 10.1 Outline-first restructuring (perfume concentrations)

Before (212 words):
> Most scents remain constant in their quality over orders of magnitude of concentration. Nevertheless, at high concentrations, quality tends to be negatively correlated with intensity, as was the case, for example, for the cinnamon oil used in this study. Hence, reliability of absolute scorings was achieved by calibrating the amount of perfume ingredients with initial ratings for intensity against a reference substance of known concentration. The final concentrations were in principal chosen in a way such that individual ratings showed variance among participants within the sliding scale between 0 and 10. This procedure seemed successful for most scents; however, the concentrations for bergamot (highest average ratings) and vetiver (lowest average rating) could probably been reduced even more, as both scents did not show any discriminating power at the level of common alleles.

Outline:
- Main idea: Were the perfume concentrations appropriate?
- Point 1: High concentrations hurt quality → not a problem because intensity was standardized.
- Point 2: Concentrations appropriate if they produce sufficient variability → true for most, with two exceptions.

After (91 words):
> Perfume intensity and quality are negatively correlated at high concentrations: if the scent is too strong, people will rate it unfavorably. Hence, we chose the final concentration of each perfume ingredient so that it had similar intensity to a reference scent (1-butanol). The resulting concentrations appeared appropriate for most scents, as participants' preferences varied along the sliding scale between 0 and 10. However, participants largely agreed on bergamot (highest average ratings) and vetiver (lowest average ratings), so lower or higher concentrations may have been needed for these scents.

### 10.2 Reorder paragraphs by outlining first (chocolate and diabetes)

Before (177 words):
> The concept of chocolate having potential therapeutic benefits for people with diabetes mellitus presents a number of intellectual challenges. It seems almost counterintuitive to suggest an energy-dense food that is high in sugar could offer such promise. However, a large volume of mechanistic and animal model studies has been undertaken demonstrating the potential benefits of cocoa and chocolate for both glucose regulation and modification of complications associated with diabetes. Cesar Fraga first proposed the potential of chocolate for people with diabetes in 2005. Grassi and colleagues further reinforced this potential for its antihypertensive and insulin-sensitizing effect. However, the hypothesis of chocolate having a beneficial effect remains counterintuitive and has yet to gain support among the wider medical and healthcare community.

Outline (reordered — lead with the evidence, end with the surprise):
1. Many studies suggest chocolate is healthy.
2. Benefits for hypertension and diabetes.
3. Medical professionals remain skeptical.
4. It is counterintuitive that chocolate might be healthy.

After (87 words):
> Many mechanistic and animal studies suggest health benefits for cocoa and chocolate, particularly for patients with hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus. These studies suggest that cocoa and chocolate can lower blood pressure, improve glucose regulation, improve insulin sensitivity, and reduce complications from diabetes. But the idea of chocolate as medicine has yet to gain widespread support among consumers or among the wider medical and healthcare community. It seems counterintuitive that a high-sugar, energy-dense food — one often seen as a treat or "dietary sin" — could promote health.

## 11. Sentence Variety Examples

### 11.1 Break monotonous structure

Before:
> The intervention reduced blood pressure. The intervention improved medication adherence. The intervention did not affect body weight. The intervention was easy to deliver. The intervention was well tolerated.

After:
> The intervention reduced blood pressure and improved medication adherence, though it did not affect body weight. Delivery was straightforward, and participants tolerated it well.

### 11.2 Vary sentence openings

Before:
> We measured cortisol levels at baseline. We measured cortisol levels at 6 weeks. We measured cortisol levels at 12 weeks. We found that cortisol decreased significantly over time.

After:
> Cortisol levels were measured at baseline, 6 weeks, and 12 weeks. Over this period, cortisol decreased significantly.

## 12. Cover Letter Example

### 12.1 Replace generic opening with a finding-led pitch

Before:
> Dear Editor, Please find attached our manuscript entitled "Obesity and Cancer Risk." We hope you will consider it for publication in your esteemed journal. Our study is about obesity and cancer. We believe it is important and timely.

After:
> Dear Editor, We are submitting our manuscript "Obesity and Site-Specific Cancer Risk in a Prospective Cohort" for consideration as an Original Article in the Journal of Clinical Oncology. We found that obesity was independently associated with higher risk of several site-specific cancers in a large prospective cohort followed for 15 years. These findings are relevant to your readership because they extend prior cross-sectional evidence with long-term prospective data. [Add originality, author-approval, and competing-interest statements only after confirming them with the authors.]

## 13. Quick Matching Guide

- If the sentence sounds bloated, look at Section 1.
- If the abstract is vague or incomplete, look at Section 2.
- If the Introduction is broad, repetitive, or missing a gap, look at Section 3.
- If the Methods are vague or hard to replicate, look at Section 4.
- If the Results read like a table, look at Section 5.
- If the Discussion starts weakly or overclaims, look at Section 6.
- If the user needs polite point-by-point revision text, look at Section 7.
- If the passage has stacked hedges or claim-strength issues, look at Section 8.
- If numbers are misleading or over-precise, look at Section 9.
- If paragraphs are disorganized and need restructuring from the outline up, look at Section 10.
- If sentences are monotonous in length or structure, look at Section 11.
- If the cover letter is generic or unfocused, look at Section 12.
