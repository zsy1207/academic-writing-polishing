## Publication integrity

### Plagiarism

Plagiarism includes:
- copying sentences or phrases from other sources
- slightly rewriting or rearranging someone else's wording
- borrowing tables or figures without proper attribution
- copying from sources such as Wikipedia into formal writing

Safe rule:
- Understand the source well enough to write from memory.
- Do not start from the original wording and merely rearrange it.
- Quote and cite only when genuine quotation is necessary.

### Self-plagiarism and duplicate publication

Watch for:
- reusing text from the author's earlier papers with only small edits
- recycling previously published data as if they were new
- submitting overlapping manuscripts to multiple journals

When polishing, flag suspicious reuse rather than smoothing it.

## Submission and revision

### Journal fit

Before or during polishing, ask:
- Is this the right audience?
- Does the manuscript match the journal's scope and format?
- Is the paper shorter and clearer than it was before?

Common submission mistakes:
- poor journal fit
- ignoring author instructions
- conclusions that go beyond the data
- clunky writing or poor data presentation

### Response to reviewers

Tone:
- grateful
- specific
- respectful
- point-by-point

Structure:
1. brief opening thanks
2. numbered reviewer comments
3. direct response after each comment
4. clear statement of what changed and where
5. reasoned explanation when declining a suggestion

Do not:
- answer emotionally
- ignore comments
- bury changes in vague language

## Peer review

### Suggested review order

1. scan the abstract
2. inspect tables and figures first
3. read the paper once through
4. inspect the Introduction
5. inspect Methods
6. inspect Results with displays open
7. inspect each table and figure directly
8. inspect the Discussion

### What to check

- Do the conclusions match the data?
- Is the paper readable without struggle?
- Is the Introduction succinct and question-driven?
- Are Methods detailed enough?
- Do Results summarize instead of transcribe?
- Are displays misleading or redundant?
- Does the Discussion overreact or wander?
- Are key references missing or misused?

### Tone for reviewer comments

- Critique the work, not the author.
- Start with a fair overview and a few positives.
- Then give numbered, specific revision points.
- Focus on major issues, not copy-editing trivia.

## Predatory-journal checks

Be cautious when a journal:
- hides or obscures contact information
- lists unverified editorial board members
- solicits aggressively by email
- hides author fees
- gives unclear peer-review information
- publishes obviously weak articles
- advertises fake or unverifiable metrics

Useful checks:
- verify editorial board affiliations
- inspect published papers
- ask prior authors about experience
- confirm membership in recognized industry associations

## Fast publishing audit

- Does any polished wording accidentally hide plagiarism or duplicate publication risk?
- Does the paper claim more than the data support?
- Is the target journal fit plausible?
- Are reviewer responses numbered and traceable?
- Are the biggest critique points about science, writing, or both?
