## Review articles

### Core job

Synthesize and evaluate the literature around a clearly defined thesis or theme.

### Rules

- Narrow the theme before drafting.
- Organize notes by section and subsection early.
- Divide the review into logical sections.
- Summarize what is known, what is contested, and what remains unknown.
- Use tables or figures when they make the synthesis easier to scan.
- Write for a broad audience unless the target venue is highly specialized.

## Grants

### General proposal habits

- Start early.
- Read the funding announcement and review criteria carefully.
- Build a writing schedule and break the work into small tasks.
- Keep the story consistent across every document in the application.
- Get feedback early and often.

### Specific aims

Key questions:
1. Why does the question matter?
2. What is the overall goal?
3. What specifically will be done?
4. What is the expected payoff?

A strong Specific Aims page:
- frames an important gap
- states a clear objective
- presents a testable hypothesis or best bet
- lists concrete aims
- ends with impact

### Research plan

Key components:
- significance and background
- aims
- methods
- expected outcomes
- possible problems and alternatives
- timeline
- future directions

Prefer strong verbs such as `will`, `test`, `determine`, and `evaluate` over weak verbs such as `hope` or `try`.

## Broader-audience science writing

### Core shift

Scientific writing often builds up to the point. Public-facing writing usually starts with the point.

### Rules

- Start with the take-home message.
- Remove jargon fast.
- Explain unfamiliar concepts step by step.
- Filter out technical detail that does not help the public audience.
- Get to the point faster than you would in a paper.
- Tell a story when the format supports it.

### Risk communication

- Prefer absolute risk to relative risk.
- Prefer whole numbers over percentages when possible.
- Give the number the audience can understand most honestly.

## Media and interviews

### When speaking with journalists

Prepare:
- 2-3 take-home messages
- the big-picture significance
- what is new
- who is affected
- key limitations

Speak:
- clearly
- concisely
- in lay language

### Helpful interview prompts

- What is the key finding?
- Why does it matter?
- How does it work?
- Who benefits?
- What surprised you?
- What made you ask this question?

## Science news structure

Typical structure:
1. lead
2. nut graf
3. early quote
4. body
5. kicker

### Lead

- short
- focused
- action-driven
- attention-grabbing without hype

### Nut graf

- explains the heart of the story
- covers the essential who, what, why, and how

### Quotes

- add human dimension, evidence, and color
- prefer `said` for attribution unless another verb is clearly justified

### Body

- explain prior context
- explain what this study did
- explain key findings
- include caveats and implications as space allows

### Kicker

- leave the reader with a satisfying close
- often circles back to the opening image or question

## Fast genre audit

- Is the text doing the right rhetorical job for this genre?
- Does the opening earn attention quickly enough?
- Are examples and stories doing real work rather than adding decoration?
- Is jargon level appropriate for the intended reader?
